If you are already renting it out, and you entered into a new tenancy on or after the 1st of April 2018, then you may be letting it unlawfully unless you have registered a valid exemption. This could attract a fine. You should take steps immediately to improve the property’s energy efficiency rating to E.

Otherwise you need to do what you can to improve the property before you enter into a new tenancy agreement, or by 1 April 2020, whichever comes soonest.

You will never be required to spend more than £3,500 (including VAT) on improving your property to an EPC E rating. If you can get your property to EPC E by spending less money, you will have met your obligation.

If it would cost more than £3,500 to improve your property to EPC E then you should install all measures which can be installed for that amount and then register an exemption. This will allow you to continue letting the property with an F or G rating.

You can include any energy efficiency improvements made since 1st October 2017 in this £3,500 cap. You can also include any third party funding, such as Green Deal finance or local authority grant funding.
